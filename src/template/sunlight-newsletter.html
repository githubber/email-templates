Sunshine Week 2014

Although we haven’t seen much spring sun in Washington recently, we’re hoping that the weather will warm up in time for the aptly named Sunshine Week. Sunshine Week is a nationwide consortium of events dedicated to the discussion of open government and the importance of access to public information – and it’s coming to a city near you! This year Sunshine Week will be held March 16-22 and will include panels and discussion across the country, so you can participate in the open government conversation no matter where you are. And for those of you in the D.C. area, Sunlight is hosting a few Sunshine Week events too. We hope to see you there!

Unlocking Government Data: Sunlight Foundation, in partnership with other open-government groups will host a panel discussion on Tuesday, March 18 at 6:30 pm at the National Press Club.

FOIA Reform: The Advisory Committee on Transparency will host a moderated discussion on Wednesday, March 19 at 2 pm at the Rayburn House Office Building, Room 2203.

The Unknown Known: Sunlight Foundation, in partnership with Participant Media, will host a free pre-release screening of "The Unknown Known" on Thursday, March 20 at 6:30 pm at Landmark’s E Street Cinema. RSVP here.
TransparencyCamp 2014

With TransparencyCamp just two months away, the TCamp team is hard at tcampwork to create another awesome event this spring. TCamp, Sunlight’s annual “unconference,” will be held on May 30-31 at the George Mason University Arlington campus. Since TCamp is an “unconference” (a discussion-based conference where the participants set the agenda), its success relies on the participation of attendees to create an engaging environment. That means we need your help! Register for TCamp14 and you can help us make this our best TCamp yet. Share the link and your ideas to help shape the conversation around TCamp14. You can even sign up to be a TransparencyCamp Ambassador and help grow the opengov community. We’ve sold out of tickets the last two years, so register for TCamp14 today – before it’s too late. We can’t wait to see what you will bring to this year’s TCamp!

How 60,000 bills tried to become law

Recently, there has been increased interest in the large proportion of bills introduced in Congress that never pass. While working on OpenCongress, we uncovered more information on the grueling and inconsistent path in which bills travel through Congress (most of which aren’t ultimately signed into law). We’ve compiled the paths of 60,000 bills introduced in the last five Congresses into one graph – and it’s not a pretty picture. Many bills bounce back and forth between the chambers and few take the familiar path through the House and Senate that we learned in civics class. This graph (and the subsequent graphs for each individual Congress) provides insight into the winding and complicated path – full of stops and starts – that most bills go through. But don’t just study this graph, start tracking bills you care about with OpenCongress and follow the complicated path a bill takes yourself!
Congressional Record Parser

Sunlight is proud to introduce our new modular Congressional-Record project. This tool allows you to parse the flat text of the Congressional Record from the Government Printing Office’s (GPO) HTML files and produce structured, bulk data that you can use. The /unitedstates project is a collaboration between open government and open source technologists to make government accountability tools more accessible to developers. So, we want feedback from you on how to make the tool as useful as possible for your project. Check out how to run the parser and then tell us how we can make the tool better for your use. You can also contribute to the Congressional-Record parser or other parts of the /unitedstates project and help us make these tools as accessible as possible for the open government community!
Transparency in the News

Change the rules on secret money
The New York Time’s Editorial Board argues that the Internal Revenue Service should proceed with its crackdown on the abuse of the tax code by political groups, despite attacks by tax-exempt nonprofits (The New York Times, February 18, 2014).

Grey Eminences: How companies try to influence government
The Economist discusses how companies influence government, claiming that the interests of producers dominate lobbying (The Economist, February 22, 2014).

Where have all the lobbyists gone?
The Nation’s Lee Fang explains how the lobbying business – that looks as if it is shrinking on paper – is thriving through underground channels (The Nation, February 19, 2014).
